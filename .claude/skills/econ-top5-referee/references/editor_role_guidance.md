# Editor Role Guidance for Economics Referee Workflows

Use this reference when `econ-aea-referee` needs an editor or editorial-board style synthesis. It condenses public guidance from AEA/AEJ/QJE sources and referee-process articles.

## Source-Based Principles

- AEA/AEJ editorial policy describes a handling editor or coeditor as the person who oversees review and makes the publication decision. The editor may desk reject without referee review when the paper is unlikely to meet journal standards, has poor fit, or lacks broad audience interest.
- AEA materials on the editorial process describe the editor, not the referee, as the final arbiter. The editor weighs evidence, uses cover letters and reports, and should read the paper enough to make an independent evaluation.
- AEA reviewer guidance emphasizes that recommendations should be based on whether conclusions are robust, credible, and worthy of publication, not on disclosure statements or personal conflicts alone.
- AEA data policy gives the handling editor responsibilities for legality, data/code, and early consultation with the AEA Data Editor or Secretary-Treasurer when data legality is in question.
- QJE describes itself as high-impact, broad in field coverage, and supported by rigorous peer review. For top general-interest journals, an editor's synthesis should judge both technical validity and importance to a broad economics audience.
- Berk, Harvey, and Hirshleifer (JEP 2017), based partly on correspondence with former editors of AER, JPE, QJE, Econometrica, ReStud, and JFE, warns that top-journal referee reports often make correctable mistakes: excessive demands, vague fatal objections, weak distinction between publishability blockers and optional improvements, and insufficient weighing of contribution against flaws.

## Editor Subagent Duties

The editor subagent should not be another referee. It should manage the review file and write a decision-oriented synthesis.

Before referees write:

1. Read the paper enough to identify field, method, contribution claim, likely journal bar, and special expertise needed.
2. Decide whether the task is a desk-review/editorial assessment or a full referee workflow.
3. Write `editor_brief.md` with:
   - paper summary and claimed contribution
   - journal/audience standard
   - referee assignments and reviewer profiles
   - specific questions each referee should answer
   - quality rules for reports
   - conflict, data, ethics, and replication checks to flag

After referee reports are submitted:

1. Audit each report for quality, professionalism, and usefulness to an editor.
2. Check whether serious objections are evidence-based and tied to publishability.
3. Distinguish consensus from disagreement across referees.
4. Identify missing expertise or whether an additional referee is needed.
5. Reject or request revision of any referee report that is vague, internally inconsistent, overly demanding, or not useful for a decision.
6. Read the paper independently enough to avoid mechanically averaging referee recommendations.
7. Write a final editorial decision memo and, when requested, a decision letter to authors.

## Final Editorial Synthesis Structure

Use this structure for the editor's final output:

1. **Manuscript and Review File**: title, authors if known, journal context, review round, referee files reviewed.
2. **Editorial Summary**: what the paper claims to contribute and why it might matter.
3. **Journal Fit and Bar**: whether the paper targets a general-interest top journal, field journal, or other venue; assess scope and audience.
4. **Referee Consensus and Disagreement**: where reports agree, where they conflict, and which disagreements matter.
5. **Independent Editorial Assessment**: editor's own view of contribution, credibility, novelty, clarity, and policy/scientific importance.
6. **Decision Recommendation**: accept, conditional accept, revise and resubmit, reject, desk reject, or seek additional referee.
7. **Required Changes for Publishability**: only items that must be addressed for publication or revision.
8. **Optional Suggestions**: useful but non-blocking improvements.
9. **Ethics, Data, Replication, and Disclosure Flags**: state none identified if no issue is evident; do not invent concerns.
10. **Draft Decision Letter** when requested: concise, consistent with the decision, and clear about required versus optional changes.

## Quality Bar for Editor Output

The editor synthesis must be decisive, not a neutral summary. It should explain why the decision follows from contribution, credibility, journal fit, and the referee evidence. It should not count votes mechanically. It should not use a weak referee report as support without auditing it.
